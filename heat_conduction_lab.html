Theory

// background · equations · concepts

🌡️ What is Heat Conduction?

Heat conduction (or thermal conduction) is the transfer of thermal energy through matter by direct contact of particles — without any bulk motion of the matter. It occurs when a temperature gradient exists in a solid or stationary fluid.

In metals, free electrons are the primary carriers of thermal energy, which is why metals are excellent conductors of both heat and electricity.

📏 Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction

The fundamental law governing heat conduction was given by Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier in 1822:

Q = −k · A · (dT / dx)
Q  = rate of heat transfer (Watts, W)
k  = thermal conductivity of material (W/m·K)
A  = cross-sectional area (m²)
dT/dx = temperature gradient along the rod (K/m)

The negative sign indicates heat flows in the direction of decreasing temperature — from hot to cold.

🧮 1D Heat Equation (Transient)

For time-dependent (transient) heat conduction in 1D without heat generation:

∂T/∂t = α · ∂²T/∂x²
α = k / (ρ·cₚ)   — thermal diffusivity (m²/s)
ρ = density (kg/m³)  ·   cₚ = specific heat capacity (J/kg·K)

In steady state, ∂T/∂t = 0, giving a linear temperature profile: d²T/dx² = 0

🔢 Finite Difference Method (Numerical)

The simulation uses the explicit finite difference scheme to approximate the PDE:

Tᵢⁿ⁺¹ = Tᵢⁿ + r·(Tᵢ₊₁ⁿ − 2Tᵢⁿ + Tᵢ₋₁ⁿ)
r = α·Δt / Δx²   (stability requires r ≤ 0.5)
i = spatial index  ·  n = time step index

⚗️ Thermal Conductivity of Common Materials

  • Silver (Ag): ~429 W/m·K — best metallic conductor
  • Copper (Cu): ~401 W/m·K — common in electronics
  • Aluminium (Al): ~237 W/m·K — lightweight conductor
  • Steel: ~50 W/m·K — structural metals
  • Glass: ~1.0 W/m·K — poor conductor / insulator
