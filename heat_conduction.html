Theory
// background · equations · concepts

🌡️ What is Heat Conduction?

Heat conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through matter via direct particle-to-particle interaction — without bulk movement of the material. It occurs whenever a temperature gradient exists across a solid or stationary fluid.

In metals, free electrons are the dominant carriers of thermal energy. This is why metals conduct both heat and electricity exceptionally well compared to non-metals.

📏 Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction

First formulated by Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier in 1822, this fundamental law states:

Q = −k · A · (dT / dx)
Q  → Heat transfer rate (Watts, W)
k  → Thermal conductivity of the material (W/m·K)
A  → Cross-sectional area perpendicular to flow (m²)
dT/dx → Temperature gradient along the rod (K/m or °C/m)

The negative sign is crucial — it indicates heat flows in the direction of decreasing temperature (from hot to cold), consistent with the Second Law of Thermodynamics.

🧮 The 1D Transient Heat Equation

For time-dependent heat conduction with no internal heat generation:

∂T/∂t = α · ∂²T/∂x²
α = k / (ρ·cₚ) → Thermal diffusivity (m²/s)
ρ = Density (kg/m³)  ·  cₚ = Specific heat (J/kg·K)

In steady state, ∂T/∂t = 0, which gives ∂²T/∂x² = 0 — meaning the temperature profile becomes perfectly linear.

🔢 Finite Difference Numerical Method

The simulation uses the explicit finite difference scheme — discretising space into N nodes and time into small steps Δt:

Tᵢⁿ⁺¹ = Tᵢⁿ + r·(Tᵢ₊₁ⁿ − 2Tᵢⁿ + Tᵢ₋₁ⁿ)
r = α·Δt / Δx²  (Stability criterion: r ≤ 0.5)
i = spatial index  ·  n = time-step index

The stability condition r ≤ 0.5 ensures the numerical solution doesn't diverge. Violating it produces oscillating, unphysical results.

⚗️ Thermal Conductivity of Common Metals

  • Silver (Ag) — 429 W/m·K: Highest of all metals; used in specialist electronics
  • Copper (Cu) — 401 W/m·K: Industry standard for heat sinks and wiring
  • Gold (Au) — 318 W/m·K: Corrosion-resistant; used in precision microelectronics
  • Aluminium (Al) — 237 W/m·K: Lightweight; ideal for aerospace and cookware
  • Steel (Fe) — ~50 W/m·K: Structural metal; poor conductor relative to other metals
  • Glass — ~1.0 W/m·K: Effective insulator; used in thermal windows
