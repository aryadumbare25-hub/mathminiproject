Aim of the Experiment
Objective · Scope · Learning Outcomes · Applications
🎯
Primary Objective
To study and simulate steady-state and transient heat conduction along a metal rod, verify Fourier's Law numerically, and analyse temperature distribution through finite-difference modelling.
🔬
Scientific Method
Employ a step-by-step guided inquiry approach — predict, simulate, observe, and compare — to build deep intuition for thermal physics beyond rote formula application.
🧮
Mathematical Foundation
Apply Engineering Mathematics concepts — partial differential equations, boundary conditions, and the finite-difference method — to a real-world heat transfer problem.
🏭
Real-world Relevance
Connect simulation findings to industrial applications: heat sinks in electronics, furnace design, building insulation, and material selection for thermal management.

📋 Learning Outcomes

LO 1Understand how thermal energy moves from hot regions to cold regions through lattice vibrations and free electron movement.
LO 2Apply Fourier's Law Q = −kA(dT/dx) to predict heat flux for given material and boundary conditions.
LO 3Observe the effect of thermal conductivity (k) on diffusion speed — and compare across materials like Copper, Steel, and Glass.
LO 4Identify steady state conditions and verify that the temperature profile becomes linear at equilibrium.
LO 5Use the explicit finite-difference method to numerically solve the 1D heat equation with Dirichlet boundary conditions.
LO 6Interpret a temperature-position graph and relate features (slope, midpoint value) to physical quantities like heat flux and boundary temperatures.
LO 7Evaluate the impact of heat pulses on a rod and observe transient redistribution before a new steady state is reached.

⚡ Industrial Applications

The principles explored in this experiment directly apply to: CPU heat sinks (copper fins rapidly conducting heat away from processors), furnace and kiln wall design (insulating materials with low k), building energy efficiency (double-glazed windows exploiting the low k of glass), soldering and welding (controlling heat spread in metal joints), and cryogenic engineering (preventing heat leakage into liquid nitrogen storage).

Theory
Background · Equations · Concepts · Numerical Methods

🌡️ What is Heat Conduction?

Heat conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct particle-to-particle interaction — without any bulk movement of the substance itself. In metals, this occurs via two mechanisms: lattice vibrations (phonons) where neighbouring atoms pass kinetic energy along, and free electron diffusion where conduction electrons carry energy much faster, explaining why metals are far better conductors than non-metals.

A temperature gradient — a difference in temperature across a distance — is the sole driving force for conduction. Without it, there is no net heat flow.

📐 Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction

Q = −k · A · (dT/dx)
Q
Heat flux (W/m²) — rate of heat transfer per unit area
k
Thermal conductivity (W/m·K) — material property
A
Cross-sectional area (m²) — perpendicular to flow
dT/dx
Temperature gradient (K/m) — driving force
−
Negative sign: heat flows opposite to gradient direction

For a rod of length L with ends at T_hot and T_cold in steady state, this simplifies to: Q = k × (T_hot − T_cold) / L

⏱️ Transient Heat Equation

∂T/∂t = α · ∂²T/∂x²
∂T/∂t
Rate of temperature change over time
α
Thermal diffusivity = k/(ρ·c_p) in m²/s
∂²T/∂x²
Spatial curvature of the temperature profile

At steady state, ∂T/∂t = 0, which means ∂²T/∂x² = 0, implying the temperature profile is perfectly linear from hot end to cold end.

🖥️ Finite Difference Method

T_i^(n+1) = T_i^n + r·(T_{i-1}^n − 2T_i^n + T_{i+1}^n)
r
r = α·Δt/Δx² — stability parameter
Δt
Time step size (must keep r ≤ 0.5)
Δx
Spatial step = L/N

Stability condition: r ≤ 0.5 must always hold. Our simulation automatically clamps α to satisfy this.

01
Set boundary conditions: T[0] = T_hot and T[N-1] = T_cold at all times (Dirichlet BCs).
02
Initialise the interior nodes — we start with a linear guess to reduce transient time.
03
At each time step, compute new T[i] using the update rule above for i = 1 to N-2.
04
Re-apply boundary conditions after every step to keep endpoints fixed.
05
Repeat until the maximum temperature change per step falls below a threshold (steady state).

⚙️ Thermal Conductivity of Common Materials

Notice the enormous range — from Silver (429 W/m·K) to Glass (≈1 W/m·K). Conductivity k determines how fast the thermal front propagates and how steep the temperature gradient becomes at steady state.
